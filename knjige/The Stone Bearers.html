I have to say, I was eagerly anticipating getting my hands on this book from the moment I first heard about it. I mean, just the raw elements alone sound like such a unique and intriguing recipe for exactly my kind story. A young adult fantasy set in a land of magic and mystery, a world governed by prophecy and overseen by powerful Stone Bearers chosen by the Gods. A land in which children travel to a city of magicians for tutelage in the fundamentals of magic, apprenticed to the different temples of the Gods to train as seers, healers, illusionists, empaths and warriors. A world in which elves and sprites, animal familiars, trickster djinni’s and dangerous demons reside. It sounds like such a delicious backdrop, the sort of world you just can’t wait to get lost in and explore.

Fortunately, I can safely say that this book delivers on so many levels. What a fast-paced, action packed, spell-binding tale, weaving together so many fantastic elements into something undeniably entertaining and wholly engaging. The Stone Bearers is a little Aladdin meets The Young Elites, and while it will undoubtedly appeal to lovers of the young adult fantasy genre it is so richly imagined and well developed that it will easily achieve broader acclaim. This is epic fantasy at its young adult best; unapologetically unputdownable fun.

The story opens to 15 year old Ashira receiving her coming-of-age prophecy, the divination that is meant to direct the course of her entire future. She has spent years dreaming of faraway places, epic adventures and noble deeds, the brave heroine of her own story, freed from an uneventful life as a potter’s daughter or someone’s wife in a remote and utterly ordinary desert village. So when she is told that she ‘will live a life of no renown’ she is devastated. Ready to choose her own fate, unconstrained by the shackles of a boring destiny, Ashira discovers a djinni’s bottle, granting her a single wish to use per day, and sets off to the city of magicians to learn magic in the hopes of proving her worth to the world. But there are whispers of a much darker prophecy in the shadows, of a demon on the rise with the power to destroy the world, and Ashira, with her mischievous and snarky djinni in tow, is inextricably tangled up in these dark forces.

I must admit I unashamedly enjoyed this novel. It’s easy to read, well-written, fairly fast-paced and flows effortlessly despite following numerous characters points of views and tying in a lot of different elements. The plot is refreshingly unique and sufficiently intricate to keep you guessing and turning the page, and while it does use some familiar and derivative elements the author succeeds in bringing something really exciting, creative and enjoyable to the well-worn fantasy table.

The cast of characters are intriguing and memorable, I really enjoyed their banter and interactions as well as the deeper friendships that developed, although I’ll admit in depth characterisation does get pushed a little to the wayside in favour of plot development. However, there is something inherently relatable about Ashira as a main character, I think a lot of readers will appreciate her determination and tenacity, her desire for something more out of life, her ambition to prove herself to the world and her frustration when her prophecy seemingly continues to control her fate. I really enjoyed the way the author utilised divination as a major component to this world, creating a real sense of threat, intrigue and momentum throughout the story but also providing this fascinating push-pull conflict between choice and destiny within the main character. I was quite impressed by how well everything managed to tie together in the end and found the way the climax of the story played out altogether unexpected but strangely fitting.

Unquestionably my favourite component to this book had to be the world-building, which is rich and vibrant and so very compelling, but underscored by this powerful sense of darkness and unease that permeates the world. I loved the magical elements of this world, from the hilariously snarky djinni with a dubious agenda to the powerful Stone Bearer’s temples presided over by the different Gods and Goddesses to the royal prince seeking out dangerous magic for unknown purposes. It is clear that a lot of attention to detail went into crafting this world, developing the magical elements and working all of the different strands of the story together in a way that is both impressive and satisfying. While the beginning of the story did feel a little abrupt and there were a few parts that felt a bit contrived, setting things up a little too perfectly or allowing characters a little too much freedom and access to certain things to be entirely believable, this is easily overlooked by the fact that the story is just so intriguing and enjoyable.

This book is, simply put, fun. In some ways light-hearted and adventurous despite delving into darker concepts, filled with magic and mystery and an honest, wholehearted sense of enthusiasm for the genre. Jacque Stevens is certainly a debut author to watch out for in the future.

On the whole a great read, well suited to the young adult/teen audience but solid enough to be enjoyed by older readers alike. I would definitely recommend giving this a go if you enjoy fantasy along the lines of Tamora Pierce, Lynette Noni or Trudi Canavan. ...more

In the kingdom of Saban each person on the cusp of adulthood can receive their own prophecy foretelling of their future. Ashira is ready for knowledge of all the adventures and grandeur that awaits her as she receives her prophecy. Unfortunately it falls short of the mark of her aspirations. She refuses to believe that it is accurate, how can someone who dreams of a life away from her duties as a potter's daughter and who believes she is destined for great adventure receive such a prophecy? "You will live a life of no renown."

Ashira is angry and escapes the moment by running outside of her city to breathe and be alone. While sitting and contemplating the dread of being just a Potter, a lady appears out of nowhere next to Ashira. This lady(Laurel) has a creature on her shoulder and questions Ashira about the seer of the city. Ashira believes it is destiny having this lady appear when and how she did. Laurel leaves in search of the seer and it is then that Ashira finds a blue bottle lying close to where Laurel was standing. Ashira picks it up and opens the cork...poof, out comes the Djinni of the bottle. He is angry, tricky and unwilling to help or cast any spell directly. (Ok I didn't tell you about the prologue of the book which sets up who the Djinni is and how he was tricked into being in the bottle. So now you get to read it to figure that out). The Djinni, or Jin which is the nickname Ashira gives him, has no memories of his former life and how he came to be in the bottle. He tricks Ashira and names her princess of a black stone after she makes a very unspecific wish for her life to be grand and adventurous. The Djinni tells her he will eventually finish the wish and give her whatever she wants when she finally figures out what that is.

This angers Ashira and she corks the bottle and heads home. Once again she comes in contact with Laurel and discovers she is a stone bearer(someone who is connected directly to one of the Gods/Goddesses and has use of their powers through a stone they give to the bearer). Ashira, through some different events, is invited to go back with Laurel to her kingdom far away and be a student at the temples of the Gods to learn about being a seer and to help better her own kingdom.

That is really the main set up of the story in a nutshell. The big question and mystery throughout the novel is a prophecy that a demon is among the bearers and will bring more demons who will bring destruction. The bearers are trying to protect all the different kingdoms from storms and demons but are not unified as well as they can be. They don't want to scare the people so are withholding some information. Ashira ends up in the middle of all the turmoil. She wants grand adventures and believes that if she can hone her magic and become connected to the bearers somehow, and with the help of her Djinni, she will change her destiny and prophecy.

The world building in the book was really interesting and good. There were a few parts that I felt it didn't quite flow as smoothly as it could have and some of the explanations didn't quite fill in the gaps needed to understand everything. The characters were fun and for the amount that were important to the story the author did a good job helping the reader to remember them all. I think some of the characters could have been developed a little bit more but as I said there were several integral characters that it probably would have made it a much longer book.

If you're wondering about the demons in the book, they are nothing scary or dark. They are more just characters that add suspense and intensity to the story. They appear a few different times and the descriptions of them aren't something that would scare younger readers. The demons are being sent by Demonic who is in search of something of his. I don't like to give spoilers so that's all you'll get on that subject. I just wanted to let anyone know who was wondering if they should let their children read this or not. As a mother I'm selective in what I let my own children read and think they would really like this. If you've read the fablehaven series the demons in here are not scary at all compared to the dark magical creatures in there.

The ending was great and really brought all the prophecies throughout the book to a close. I kept wondering how Ashira's prophecy would pan out. Would she keep it, or would she be able to change her own destiny? Did she want to change her destiny after all that happened to her or did she want to stay a person of no renown. The author did a good job of showing the possibility of Ashira wanting either or and keeping the reader trying to guess which one she will ultimately choose.

Ashira and the Djinni's relationship is fun to watch develop. They learn through many mistakes, arguments and adventures of how to help each other.
